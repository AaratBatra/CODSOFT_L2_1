Sir Isaac Newton

Physicist, Mathematician, and Astronomer

Sir Isaac Newton (1642–1727) was an English physicist, mathematician, and astronomer. He is widely recognized as one of the most influential scientists of all time. Newton made groundbreaking contributions to various fields of science.

His most famous work includes the laws of motion, the law of universal gravitation, and the development of calculus. These contributions laid the foundation for modern physics and mathematics and significantly advanced our understanding of the natural world.



Works of Sir Issac Newton

Laws of motion

First Law of Motion: An object will continue moving (or staying still) unless acted upon by an external force.
Second Law of Motion: Force = Mass x Acceleration.
Third Law of Motion: When one body exerts a force on a second body, the second body simultaneously exerts a force equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to that of the first body.

Law of Gravitation

Discovery: Using this law and making extrapolations based on it, Newton derived Kepler’s empirical laws of planetary motion, which naturally emerged from his gravitational theory.
Universal Law of Gravitation: F = G {(M1 * M2)/d^2}
Theory: Newton's most famous discovery was the law of universal gravitation, which states that every object in the universe attracts every other object with a force that is directly proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. This fundamental law laid the foundation for our understanding of gravity and planetary motion, revolutionizing physics.

Optics

Newton and Optics: Sir Isaac Newton made significant contributions to the field of optics during the late 17th century. His groundbreaking work on optics is encapsulated in his book "Opticks," first published in 1704
The Nature of Light: Newton conducted experiments that demonstrated that white light is composed of a spectrum of colors, which we now know as the visible spectrum. He passed a beam of sunlight through a glass prism, causing it to disperse into the colors of the rainbow, revealing the fundamental properties of light.
Reflective and Refractive Properties: Newton also extensively studied the reflection and refraction of light. He formulated laws governing these phenomena, including the law of reflection and the law of refraction (Snell's Law), which describe how light changes direction when it encounters different mediums.
